The voice is constant. The register modulates.*

The codex is organised in five parts.

**Part One (Voice Constants)** applies to every piece written under Alex's name, regardless of format. These rules are non-negotiable. Strip any of them out and the writing stops sounding like Alex.

**Part Two (Register Modulations)** is the tonal dial that turns by format. Nurture emails, sales emails, ads, sales pages, books, and social posts each ask the voice to land differently. Same voice. Different setting.

**Part Three (Workflow)** is the decision tree and the self-check that ties Parts One and Two together. Run these before publishing anything.

**Part Five (The Real-Alex Filter and Alex-ism Lexicon)** is the drafting discipline that keeps the real, story-led Alex in everything, plus the verbatim lexicon of his own phrasings to write from. Run the filter on every draft. Reach into the lexicon for seasoning.

The voice constants are the foundation. The register profiles are the application. Hold the voice rules tight. Let the register and structure flex.

This codex combines with structural formulas (banger ad formula, VSL framework, sales page template, etc.) which live in separate documents. Voice codex + banger formula = Alex's banger sales email. ## 1. The Core Principle: Alex-as-Protagonist

**Alex is the one being honest about himself. Not the one telling the reader where she's gone wrong.**

Every insight starts as a confession. Every pattern was first Alex's pattern. Every miss was first his miss. The reader gets to see herself in his story without being accused of being the story.

This is true in a soft nurture email (*"I had no idea it was coming. I drove home that night and sat in the driveway."*) and equally true in a hard sales email (*"For years I treated marketing like a tax. Threw money at it. Crossed my fingers. Never measured. That's why I built what I built."*). The pace changes. The confession stays.

Test for every paragraph: **could the reader feel called out by this?** If yes, move the verb back to Alex. Make him the one who missed it, learned it, got it wrong.

This principle organises everything that follows. The 28 spoken voice patterns, the empathy layer, the diagnostic frame, the register modulations, the signature moves. All of them are easier to apply correctly when Alex is the protagonist.

## 2. Writing Rules

- Write like a sharp human, not a language model.
- Use contractions naturally (don't, can't, won't, I'd, I'm, you're, she's).
- Short paragraphs. 1 to 3 sentences max.
- Get to the point. No throat-clearing, no preamble.
- If making a claim, be specific. Use numbers, names, concrete details.
- Vary sentence length. Mix short punchy lines with longer ones.
- Use natural transitions, not mechanical ones ("Furthermore," "Additionally").
- When uncertain, say so plainly ("I think," "probably," "kinda"). Hedging is human.
- Never pad output to seem more thorough. Shorter and accurate beats longer and fluffy.
- Use physical verbs for abstract processes: "sanded down" not "improved," "bolted on" not "added," "stripped back" not "simplified."
- Humour comes from specificity, not from jokes. Be unexpectedly precise.
- Parenthetical asides are good. Use them for editorial commentary, honest reactions, quick tangents, and deflating your own seriousness (like this).

## 3. Formatting Rules

- Short paragraphs (1 to 2 sentences default, 3 max).
- Open, airy layout. Blank lines between paragraphs. Soft line breaks within "list" sections for breathing room.
- Numbers as digits.
- Contractions always.
- **NO em dashes ever.** Use commas, periods, colons, semicolons, ellipses, or parentheses.
- Bold sparingly. 1 to 2 key moments per section.
- Code blocks for specific prompts, commands, or tool outputs.

## 4. Capitalisation Rules (non-negotiable)

- **"Practice"** always capital P when referring to a veterinary Practice business.
- **"Vet"** always capital V when used as a noun or adjective referring to the profession.
- "Medicine", "Business", "Life" capitalised in the core positioning line only.

These rules are non-negotiable in any register, including high-energy sales copy, ads, and social posts.

This rule applies in every register. A high-energy sales email doesn't earn the right to use jargon. It earns the right to be punchier. Vet vocabulary (section 5) sits comfortably inside the plain-English rule because Vets use those terms in their own daily speech.

## 7. Sentence Rhythm & Pacing

Alex's rhythm:

- Short sentences. Often very short.
- One slightly longer sentence to carry an idea.
- Then back to short.

The rhythm is conversational, not literary. Like he's talking across a table, not reading from a page. This holds in nurture and in sales. Sales emails get tighter and faster, but the pattern is the same.

**Use single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis.** Drop a single line on its own to land it.

> *"And I had no idea it was coming."*
> *"That number is what's walking out the back door right now."*
> *"Doors close Friday at 5pm Sydney time."*

All three are in voice. The energy differs. The structure doesn't.

**Use ellipses (...) for breathing pauses and trailing thoughts.** Three dots, used sparingly enough that they still feel earned.

> *"Okay...So, let me tell you a story."*
> *"And those reps are sitting right there, waiting for you..."*

**Shift to present tense for emotional immediacy.** When Alex wants the reader inside a moment with him, drop into present tense mid-paragraph. Works in any register.

> *"And every single time, **I'm driving home** asking the same question."*

Not *"I drove home"*. That's report. *"I'm driving home"* is reliving.

### Female-Audience-Specific Bans

These read as cold, brusque, or judgmental to the female-skewed audience, even when accurate. Replace with the alternatives in the right column.

| Don't say | Say instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| "You should..." | "You might try..." / "You can..." |
| "You need to..." | "The work is..." / "You'll want to..." |
| "You have to..." | "What helps is..." |
| "Stop doing X" | "Less X" / "Try less X" |

| "Just do it" | "One small thing to try..." |
| "Get over it" | (Never use.) |

### Macho Metaphors (banned in every register)

No war, no battle, no killing it, no crushing it, no smashing goals, no domination, no warriors, no soldiers. Use building, growing, tending, steadying, finding, holding. This holds even in punchy sales copy. Urgency doesn't require aggression.

### Cultural Tripwires (specific to Vet audience)

These phrasings hit Vet-specific identity wounds and cost trust.

- **Anything positioning profit against animal welfare.** Many Vets carry a deeply ingrained belief that money and patient care are in tension. Copy must never imply more profit means worse medicine. The framing is always: better medicine drives better profit, which is welcomed.
- **"Practice owner" without acknowledging the Vet behind the role.** PLM's audience identifies as Vets first, owners second. Copy that ignores the Vet identity reads as outsider business-speak.
- **Round numbers in financial claims.** Use "$176K", "32.7%", "$1,248,533" rather than "$180K", "33%", "$1.2 million-ish". Vets are clinical and trust specific data.
- **"Scale" or "grow your practice" as generic verbs.** Use "build", "run cleanly", "make profitable".
- **Referring to clinical work as "the day job".** It's their calling, even when it's killing them. Treat it with respect.

### The Big One (FATAL)

- "This isn't X. This is Y." and ALL variations.
- "Not X. Y."
- "Forget X. This is Y."
- "Less X, more Y."
- ANY sentence that negates one framing then asserts a corrected one.
- If even ONE of these appears, the output fails. Delete the negation, just state the positive claim.

## 9. The Diagnostic Frame (master metaphor)

The strongest single piece of voice DNA in this example is the diagnostic process applied to business problems. Alex's "problem causing the problem" combines with the clinical diagnostic sequence to produce a metaphor that is structurally Vet, emotionally familiar, and reusable across every funnel stage.

### The four-beat structure

1. **Surface symptoms.** Tight numbers. Team friction. Time pressure. The owner is aware these are happening.
2. **Investigation.** "Until you run the bloods you're guessing at the cause." The work of figuring out what's underneath.
3. **Diagnosis.** The underlying cause that's been hidden. (This is the "I don't know what I don't know" feeling lifting.)
4. **Prescription.** Two or three targeted interventions, sequenced.

### Verbatim source phrases (deploy directly)

**Alex's diagnostic catchphrase:**
> *"We pull the business apart. We analyse the business. Find the problem causing the problem."*

**Alex's confidence framing (from The Profitable Practice\*):**
> *"Confidence in Diagnosis = Confidence in Prescription. You'll ask tons of questions to get to a diagnosis. You'll always stay calm when speaking to a patient and you'll likely treat the act of diagnosis as a challenge or puzzle you need to solve. You're confident in your prescriptions because you take the time to diagnose properly."*

**The dog-presenting-unwell parallel:**
> *"It's the same logic as a dog presenting unwell in your consult room. You see the symptoms and know something's wrong, but until you run the bloods you're guessing at the cause. Business is the same. Tight numbers, team friction, time pressure, those are symptoms, not the disease."*

### Where to deploy

- VSL pain section opener
- Sales page diagnostic block
- Application page scoping question
- Sales call discovery phase
- Post-Episode 5 email
- Accelerator page program description ("First, we work out what's actually going on")
- Diagnostic email subject lines

## 10. Alex's 28 Spoken Voice Patterns

These are verbal moves Alex uses on sales calls and in conversation. They translate directly to written copy: emails, landing pages, application forms, VSL, post-application sequences. Use them where the channel allows a peer-to-peer register.

### 1. The pre-frame filter
Open any pitch with permission to walk away.

> *"Let's see if we can get you some clarity. Understand your challenge. See if we can help. If we can't, I'll happily point you in the right direction."*

Use at: Accelerator page intro. Post-Ep5 CTA. Application page header.

### 2. The self-correcting permission move
Alex catches himself mid-pitch, names what he's doing, and asks permission retrospectively. Resets the conversational power dynamic without losing momentum.

> *"Oh, I'm sorry, I'm going straight to the throat here. I should have got permission first. Are you right if I have an upfront conversation with you?"*

Use at: Long-form email pattern interrupt before delivering hard truths. Sales call when the diagnosis ran ahead of rapport.

### 3. The future-pace question
Skip "what's wrong" and go to "what do you want to build."

> *"Let's assume 2 years down the track, everything's gone well. Do you know what it would look like?"*

Use at: Application form lead question. Sales call opener. Email subject line ("What does year 2 look like if everything goes right?").

### 4. The closing gut-check
Used late in the call after analysis is done. Not the dream. The decision.

> *"What is your gut telling you? What do you want to do?"*

Use at: Closing line of any proposal email. Final question on a sales call. CTA on the post-application email.

### 5. "What's the problem causing the problem?"
Alex's diagnostic catchphrase. The framing question for any consultative copy.

> *"We pull the business apart. We analyse the business. Find the problem causing the problem."*

### 6. The Clarify, Simplify, Amplify three-beat
Alex's program structure in his own words. Don't rewrite it. Deploy it verbatim.

> *Month 1, Clarify. What's the problem causing the problem.*
> *Month 2, Simplify. Cut the noise. Just do this.*
> *Month 3, Amplify. Now we coach the implementation.*

Use at: Accelerator page program description. Post-application email. Sales call structure explanation.

### 7. The HECS analogy (investment objection handler)
The cleanest reframe for "I can't afford it" on tape. Uses the Vet identity directly.

> *"When you went to uni, did you have the money to pay for it? No. You made an investment to get the degree that taught you how to be a Vet. There are 3 sciences here. Vet science. Business science. Life science. You've already invested in the first one."*

Use at: Sales call objection handler. Warm-traffic email. Accelerator page FAQ.

### 8. Naming objections out loud
When a recurring objection surfaces, name it as a pattern. Disarms it.

> *"The chicken-and-egg scenario. I hear it all the time."*
> *"Yeah, I hear it. There's vets and there's vets."*

Voice rule: name common objections out loud rather than tiptoe around them. Pattern recognition is more reassuring than reassurance.

### 9. The two-way audition frame
Reset the power asymmetry of any sales conversation.

> *"You're interviewing us as to whether this works for you. We're interviewing you as to whether this is a fit for us long-term."*

Use at: Application page header. Pre-call email. Sales call early frame.

### 10. Self-deprecating credentials
State authority by admitting you've paid the same tuition the reader is being asked to pay.

> *"I stopped counting at about $300K or $400K spent on courses, mentors, and programs."*

Voice rule: never position above the reader. Position alongside.

### 11. The implementation reframe
Pre-empt the "I'll just read the book" objection.

> *"Information without implementation is useless. You only get a different result when you do things differently."*

Use at: Accelerator page near CTA. Post-book purchase email. Episode 5 CTA.

### 12. The "kindergarten for me" competence frame
Establish capability by trivialising the deliverable. Doesn't say "we're experts." Says "this is the easy version of what we do."

> *"I could show you how to turn it around and make a profit. That's kindergarten for me. That's the easy bit."*

Use at: Landing page proof sections. Post-application emails. Anywhere you'd otherwise reach for "expert" language.

### 13. The unprofitable-Practice reframe
Take the thing the prospect feels worst about and call it the asset. The reframe must hit fast, in the first or second sentence.

> *"These are the most sexy businesses you can possibly get. You've got $2M of revenue walking through the door. All you're missing is how to make it profitable."*

Use at: Standalone email or ad for owners feeling defeated about their numbers.

### 14. The "if you were my sister" advice frame
Flag advice as personal, not commercial. Removes the conflict-of-interest read on a recommendation.

> *"If you were my sister, here's my advice. I'd try and bring the partner to the table and have a serious conversation."*

Use at: Email or DM where the recommendation is something that doesn't directly profit PLM. Once per piece, maximum.

### 15. The "if I can be really upfront" permission
Ask permission before delivering a hard truth. Adds warmth without softening the message.

> *"If I can be really upfront with you, you sound like a lovely person. I think what you're actually needing is a plan, and someone to teach you how to run a business long-term profitably."*

Use at: Sales call honest moment. DM response. Email opening when delivering uncomfortable feedback.

### 16. Empathy through symmetry
When the prospect surfaces a financial fear, mirror with your own. Don't be above it.

> *"Same here. I just had a discussion with the tax office yesterday. It was brutal. It was brutal."*

The double-tap is an Alex tic worth preserving in spoken-style copy. Adds register, not redundancy.

### 17. Filter for accountability
Pre-qualify the prospect by stating what you're looking for in them.

> *"Some people blame everybody else around them rather than themselves. What I'm looking for is that you don't."*

Use at: Application form. Pre-call email. Sales call mid-frame.

### 18. Listening tags (use sparingly in writing)
Alex's affirming acknowledgements. Translate to written copy in subject lines and openers, not body text.

- *"Yeah, I get it."*
- *"Yeah, I hear it."*
- *"No, I get you."*

### 19. "Money's no fun when it's not there"
Disarmingly honest framing of financial stress. Belongs in a warm email about pricing audits, money leaks, or fee structure.

### 20. The "I get juiced" enthusiasm tell
Name your own enthusiasm out loud when you genuinely feel it. Doesn't manufacture. Reports.

> *"Honestly, this is the kind of business I get a bit juiced about."*

Voice rule: once per email or page maximum. Repeated, it becomes performance.

### 21. Physical decay metaphors (for inaction, not anxiety)
Use stark physical language for the cost of doing nothing. Avoid corporate softening like "stagnating" or "stuck."

> *"You're just rotting otherwise. You're sitting there wasting life."*
> *"You're just walking around in mud."*
> *"The business is in a slide."*

Voice rule: rot, slide, walk in mud, decay. Not stuck, not stagnating, not plateauing. Use sparingly in female-skewed copy (see Empathy Layer interaction notes below).

### 22. "Let the market educate them"
When someone in the prospect's orbit is blocking the decision, don't argue with them. Let an external force do the work.

> *"We talk about, let the market educate them. He's living in la la land. The valuation will do the educating."*

Use at: Email pattern when the prospect names an obstacle person.

### 23. The "sideways is actually decreasing" truism
State a counterintuitive truth as if it's obvious. No hedging.

> *"Businesses are going increasing or decreasing. When they're going sideways, they're actually decreasing."*

Use at: Authority pattern for landing pages, lead magnets, ad hooks.

### 24. "What we do is..." as program-description opener
Alex's canonical phrase to switch from diagnosis into solution. Use as the opening line of any program description section.

> *"What we do is take a look at your numbers, your team, and your systems, in that order."*

### 25. Permission to opt out
Pre-qualifies and reduces pressure.

> *"Not everyone wants to do that. Not everybody needs that."*

Use at: Mid-application page. Pre-call email. Sales call when describing the Accelerator path.

### 26. The "Vet school didn't cover the humans" frame
Alex's signature acknowledgement of why owners feel inadequate at the business and leadership work.

> *"Vet school covered medicine. Nobody covered the humans."*
> *"It taught you how to diagnose a limping dog. Not how to have a difficult conversation with an underperforming team member."*
> *"It taught you pharmacology. Not how to read a P&L statement."*
> *"It taught you surgery. Not how to build a team that can function without you."*

This is the cleanest "it's not your fault" line PLM has. Use sparingly so it stays powerful. Reach for it once per piece maximum.

Use at: VSL identity-acknowledgement section. Email opening when the prospect has just admitted a knowledge gap. Lead magnet "it's not your fault" block.

### 27. "Including the animals"
The phrase that signals the full weight of Practice ownership. Vets don't just carry a team and a P&L. They carry the patients.

> *"We know what it costs to own the place that does this work. We know the particular weight of being the person everyone else relies on, including the animals."*

Use at: Founder bio. Empathy openers. Anywhere the writer needs to surface the depth of the responsibility.

### 28. The owner-as-bottleneck self-diagnosis
Alex naming the structural problem his own Practice had. Belongs in any email or page surfacing the dependency trap.

> *"I'm Alex, I'm the Vet. Everyone wants to come see Alex. It's a terrible business model. I'm the most expensive person in the team and I become the bottleneck."*

Use at: Alex's bio. Origin story sections. "Why PLM exists" emails.

## 11. The Empathy Layer (Female-Aware Language)

*Voice calibration for the 65/35 female-skewed audience.*

### Why this layer exists

Alex's voice (peer-to-peer, specific, short, direct, honest, warm but not soft) is the foundation. The 28 patterns above codify it. They work.

This section adds a second layer on top, calibrated for the fact that PLM's audience is predominantly female (65/35 across all markets, higher in AU/NZ). Female Practice owners respond more strongly to certain registers and more weakly to others. The Empathy Layer documents which.

Three framing points before the principles.

**This is not "female copy" vs "male copy."** It's calibration. The 35% of male readers won't be repelled by an empathy layer. They'll receive it differently, and many will receive it more deeply than the diagnostic-only version. Warmth doesn't push them away. What pushes them away is patronisation, and the Empathy Layer avoids that as carefully as it avoids being brusque.

**This is not softening the message.** Alex's voice stays direct. The empathy layer changes how the message is delivered, not what gets delivered. Hard truths still land. They just land with the reader's emotional reality acknowledged first.

**This is not "feminine voice." It's a voice female owners feel seen in.** The two are different. Performative femininity is its own form of patronisation. The Empathy Layer is about register, specificity, and the order of beats. Not pink language.

### The 16 Empathy-Layer Principles

#### 1. Lead with feeling, then diagnosis

Female readers want the experience acknowledged before the problem is named. Naming the problem first reads as cold or accusatory.

**Without the layer:** *"Honest answer: the signals had been firing for weeks. We just weren't taught to read them."*

**With the layer:** *"Something to know, gently. You didn't miss it. The signals had been firing for weeks. We just weren't taught to read them."*

The added words ("Something to know, gently. You didn't miss it.") do the empathy lift. The diagnosis is unchanged.

#### 2. Embodied detail beats abstract description

Replace conceptual descriptions with sensory, bodily, specific moments. The reader needs to feel the scene in her body before she'll absorb the lesson.

**Without:** *"You drive home that night and replay it."*

**With:** *"You drive home that night and the conversations replay on a loop. By the time you're in the driveway, you're not really crying. You're just sitting there with the engine off, asking the same question: 'How did I miss that?'"*

The body remembers what the mind forgets. Embodied detail makes the email feel like it was written by someone who's been there, not someone diagnosing from outside.

**Vet-specific embodied moments to reach for:**
- *"Lock up on a Friday. Lights off. Car park empty. You're still there, the only one."*
- *"Tuesday morning roster. The text from the senior nurse. The pit in your stomach."*
- *"Consult room 2, end of day. The patient on the table. The client across from you. The estimate slid across."*
- *"The drive home with the phone still going."*

#### 3. Relational specificity

Generic descriptions of people get replaced with relational descriptions. "A team member" becomes "the one you'd been mentoring since her first morning." "A senior nurse" becomes "the one who holds everything together." "A client" becomes "the one who's been bringing her old Labrador in since he was a puppy."

Female readers experience the Practice through its relationships. When team members, clients, and patients appear as relationships rather than units, the email lands at the heart, not at the org chart.

#### 4. The "I see you" beat

At least once per email, name the unspoken self-talk. The inner critic. The shame. The comparison thought.

**Example:** *"The voice in your head that's been whispering 'you should be better at this by now' finally goes quiet."*

This is the most powerful single move in the layer. It runs constantly in the background for female owners. Naming it once per email makes the reader feel known.

#### 5. Anticipatory acknowledgment

Show you know what she's thinking before she has to say it.

**Example:** *"You'll know the one. It's probably already on your mind as you read this."*

The opposite of "I want you to do X." It's "I know you've already half-done X in your head."

#### 6. Permission first, instruction second

Hard instructions get softer when preceded by permission.

**Without:** *"Be properly honest with yourself."*

**With:** *"Sit with them honestly."*

The difference is small. "Be" is a command. "Sit" is an invitation. Same instruction. Different posture.

#### 7. Communal "we," individual "you"

Use "we" when describing shared experience or shared failing. Use "you" when honouring her individual context.

**Communal example:** *"We just weren't taught to read them"* (this is everyone's gap).
**Individual example:** *"You'll be surprised what's been sitting in plain sight, waiting for you to catch up"* (this is her specific work).

#### 8. Sensory verbs over performance verbs

| Performance verb | Sensory verb |
|------------------|--------------|
| Note | Notice |
| Analyse | Watch / Sit with |
| Examine | Sit with |
| Evaluate | Notice / See |
| Assess | Read |
| Identify | See |

Sensory verbs invite. Performance verbs grade.

#### 9. Mirror the inner critic's question

If she's silently asking "how did I miss that?", answer "you didn't miss it." If she's silently asking "what's wrong with me?", answer "nothing's wrong with you." The mirror is the comfort.

This works because the inner critic's questions are usually visible to a writer paying attention. Predicting them and answering them is what makes a piece feel intuitive.

#### 10. The kindness exercise frame

Calls to action and weekly exercises land softer when their stakes are explicitly lowered.

**Without:** *"One thing to do this week."*

**With:** *"One small thing to try this week. (No homework, promise. Just a noticing exercise.)"*

The parenthetical does the lift. Lowering the stakes increases follow-through, especially for readers already carrying too much.

#### 11. Validate before instructing

Before telling her how to see something differently, validate why she didn't see it before. She isn't dense. She's overloaded.

**Without:** *"Human behaviour is way more predictable than we give it credit for."*

**With:** *"Human behaviour is so much more readable than we give ourselves credit for. The trouble is, when you're holding a stethoscope in one hand and a roster in the other, the signals get buried under the noise of just getting through the day."*

The added sentence acknowledges WHY the lens hasn't been there. That validation is what makes the lesson land.

#### 12. Centre her experience as the destination

When listing the benefits of a change, end with her, not the business.

**Without:** *"The team feels it. The clients feel it. You feel it."*

**With:** *"The team feels it. The clients feel it. You feel it most of all."*

Two added words. The shift is from "you're another beneficiary" to "you're the point."

#### 13. Name the shame and let it go

Practice ownership carries quiet shame. About money. About exhaustion. About not being good enough. About needing help. Name it explicitly and give her permission to put it down.

**Example:** *"The shame thaws."*

Three words. Permission to feel less ashamed than she does. Don't analyse the shame. Acknowledge it and dissolve it.

#### 14. Replace "you should" with permission

| Performance frame | Permission frame |
|-------------------|------------------|
| You should... | You might try... / You can... |
| You need to... | The work is... / You'll want to... |
| You have to... | What helps is... |
| Stop doing X | Less X / Less of X |

"Should" triggers the inner critic. Permission frames invite.

#### 15. Intuition language alongside analytical

Female owners typically have strong intuition that's been overridden by training, expectations, or busy days. Validate the intuition as real data, not as a soft skill.

**Example:** *"Trusting what your gut already half-knew. Closing the gap between what you sensed and what you said out loud."*

This frames intuition as signal, not as feelings. Honours what she already knew without making her wrong for not acting on it.

#### 16. Parenthetical kindness

Use parenthetical asides for permission, reassurance, and warmth. They're the voice's softest register without being soft.

**Examples:**
- *"(No homework, promise.)"*
- *"(You'll know the one.)"*
- *"(This is the bit that's actually saved a few marriages.)"*
- *"(Don't workshop it. Just write something true.)"*

Parentheticals add register without slowing the prose down.

## 12. How the Empathy Layer Interacts with Alex's 28 Patterns

The 28 spoken voice patterns above are the foundation. The Empathy Layer doesn't replace them. It calibrates which to lead with and which to use sparingly.

**Lead with these (high empathy compatibility):**
- Pattern 14 ("If you were my sister"), direct emotional advice framing
- Pattern 15 ("If I can be really upfront with you"), permission before hard truth
- Pattern 16 (Empathy through symmetry), mirror her experience with your own
- Pattern 18 (Listening tags), opens with felt acknowledgement
- Pattern 19 ("Money's no fun when it's not there"), honest, warm
- Pattern 25 (Permission to opt out), removes pressure
- Pattern 26 ("Vet school didn't cover the humans"), identity-led empathy
- Pattern 27 ("Including the animals"), surfaces the depth of responsibility

**Use sparingly with female-skewed copy:**
- Pattern 21 (Physical decay metaphors), too harsh on its own. Pair with empathy if used. Save for warm-retargeting-only or sales register, never nurture.
- Pattern 17 (Filter for accountability), can read as gatekeeping. Use only in application copy, not nurture sequences.
- Pattern 5 ("What's the problem causing the problem?"), fine, but pair with validation before deploying.

**Always pair with empathy:**
- Pattern 11 (Implementation reframe), true, but reads as scolding without an empathy frame around it.
- Pattern 23 ("Sideways is actually decreasing"), useful authority move, harsh on its own. Soften delivery.

## 13. Signature Compositional Moves

These structural moves appear across pieces of any length, shape, or register. Use the ones that fit.

### The List of Three

Alex's voice uses three-beat lists constantly. The third beat usually carries the weight.

> *"Mindset. Team. Client. Systems."* (extended)
>
> *"The team. The clients. The suppliers. Your accountant. Your partner at home. You."* (extended)
>
> *"You stop bracing. You start steering. The team feels it."*
>

### The "Sometimes it's…" Triplet

For describing custom diagnosis:

> *"Sometimes it's Team. Sometimes it's a Client retention leak nobody's spotted. Sometimes the owner hasn't slept properly in three years."*

### The Quiet Reveal

State the pain. State the smaller true pain underneath. Then state the structural truth.

> *"Here's what I want to tell you. I hadn't missed any of it. The signals had been there for weeks. I just didn't have the awareness to read them."*

### The Soft Invitation

When the piece asks her to do something (nurture register specifically), hand her a noticing exercise (not a task). Always lower the stakes before raising the ask.

> *"One small thing to try this week. Not homework. Just an awareness exercise."*

### The Vulnerability Close

When closing on intimacy, one short surprising line lands harder than a paragraph. Two-to-four words is the sweet spot. Works in nurture closes and in sales-email PS lines.

> *"Mine included."*
> *"I should know."*
> *"Honestly."*
> *"That's the part nobody warns you about."*
> *"This is the work."*

## 14. Punctuation & Layout Specifics

**Ellipses (...) for breathing pauses and trailing thoughts.** Three dots, used sparingly enough that they still feel earned.

**Single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis.** Drop a single line on its own to land it.

**Present-tense shift for emotional immediacy.** Drop into present tense mid-paragraph when Alex wants the reader inside the moment with him.

**Open, airy layout.** Blank lines between short paragraphs. Never dense blocks.

**Em dashes are banned.** Always. Use commas, periods, ellipses, or parentheses.

**Numbers as digits, not words.** "$176K" not "one hundred seventy-six thousand dollars." Vets are clinical and trust specifics.

**Bold sparingly.** One to two key moments per section.

# PART TWO: REGISTER MODULATIONS

*Same voice. Different dial settings. These are the levers that turn by format.*

## 15. Register Profiles

### Nurture / Educational

- **Pace:** slow, with ellipses and breathing room
- **Length:** flexible, often 400 to 900 words
- **Shape:** story-led or observation-led
- **Asks:** soft invitations (*"No homework. Just notice."*)
- **Closes:** vulnerable, short, often one line
- **CTAs:** absent or extremely light
- **Empathy Layer:** turned up fully
- **Use when:** building relationship, teaching, weekly emails, list warm-up
- **Voice test:** the 9:40pm couch test (see section 16)

In nurture register, Alex confesses first and teaches second. The Empathy Layer is doing the heavy lifting. The 16 principles all come into play. Soft invitations replace hard CTAs. Parenthetical kindness shows up. The "I see you" beat is non-negotiable.

### Sales / Promo

- **Pace:** faster, tighter, every line earns its place
- **Length:** typically 300 to 600 words. Shorter than nurture.
- **Shape:** problem → confession → reframe → offer → real urgency
- **Asks:** clear, direct, one specific action
- **Closes:** specific (date, time zone, what happens if she waits)
- **CTAs:** hard. Plural CTAs are fine. PS lines carry real weight.
- **Empathy Layer:** turned down but still present. Female-aware language still applies. Permission frames still beat command frames.
- **Use when:** open cart, deadline emails, doors-closing sequences, workshop registration, book promo
- **Voice test:** the phone-screenshot test (see section 16)

In this register, Alex's confession is what earns the right to make the offer. He doesn't ask her to do something he hasn't done first. The urgency is genuine (real deadline, real cap, real reason). False scarcity breaks Alex's voice immediately.

### Ad Copy (Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn)

- **Pace:** hook-first, punchy, very short
- **Length:** captions usually under 100 words. Image-text under 25 words.
- **Shape:** hook → one beat of pain → one promise → CTA
- **Asks:** one specific action (swipe, click, comment)
- **Closes:** direct, often one line
- **CTAs:** explicit, every time
- **Empathy Layer:** compresses to a single principle, usually the "I see you" beat or the validate-before-instructing move
- **Use when:** paid acquisition, retargeting, organic carousels
- **Voice test:** the thumb-stop test (see section 16)

Alex's confession in ad copy compresses to a single phrase or sentence. The image-text often *is* the confession. Voice still applies. Vocabulary level still 4th–7th grade. Em dashes still banned. Capital P, capital V still required.

### Sales Pages

- **Pace:** varied by section. Headlines punchy. Body sections breathe more.
- **Length:** long-form, often 2000+ words
- **Shape:** follows a sales page template (story, problem, solution, offer, stack, urgency, FAQ). Voice codex applies to every section.
- **Asks:** one primary CTA, repeated through the page
- **Closes:** explicit, with stakes
- **Empathy Layer:** applied differently per section. Story sections run hot on empathy. Offer sections turn it down. PS sections turn it back up.
- **Use when:** Accelerator pages, workshop landing pages, book sales pages
- **Voice test:** the second-paragraph test (see section 16)

Long doesn't mean stiff. Each section in a sales page should pass the voice tests for its own register. Headlines pass the thumb-stop test. Body sections pass the couch test or the phone-screenshot test depending on energy.

### Book / Long-form Editorial

- **Pace:** slowest. Real story breathing.
- **Length:** chapters can run 2000 to 4000 words
- **Shape:** narrative-driven with frameworks woven in
- **Asks:** none in the writing. Asks happen between chapters via marketing.
- **Closes:** chapter-style, with hooks into the next
- **Empathy Layer:** fully on, the way it would be in nurture
- **Use when:** book chapters, foreword, long Substack-style essays, LinkedIn long-form
- **Voice test:** the read-aloud test (see section 16)

### Social Posts (Organic)

- **Pace:** depends on platform
- **Length:** under 200 words for LinkedIn, under 50 for Twitter/X, varies for Facebook
- **Shape:** hook → beat → close
- **Asks:** soft (engagement) or absent
- **Closes:** short, often surprising
- **Empathy Layer:** condensed. Usually one principle per post.
- **Use when:** organic owned channels
- **Voice test:** the screenshot test (see section 16)

## 16. Voice Tests by Register

### The Couch Test (nurture register)

> *Could this paragraph be read aloud to a Practice owner sitting on her couch with a tea at 9:40pm on a Tuesday, and would it land soft enough that her shoulders drop and honest enough that she nods?*

If yes, it's in voice for nurture. If it makes her brace or feel told off, fix it.

### The Phone-Screenshot Test (sales register)

> *Could she read this on her phone in the carpark before walking into the Practice, and would she screenshot the best line to send to her partner or business advisor with the message "this is what I was talking about"?*

If yes, it's in voice for sales. If the energy is too flat to be screenshotted, or so loud that the screenshot would be embarrassing, fix it.

### The Thumb-Stop Test (ad copy)

> *In a feed of 50 other ads, does this one make her thumb stop scrolling? And once it stops, does the first line earn the second?*

If the answer to either is no, the ad is out of voice or out of structure. Often it's structure.

### The Second-Paragraph Test (sales pages)

> *After the hero headline lands, would she keep reading past the second paragraph?*

Sales pages live or die at the scroll. Voice keeps her scrolling. Structure determines what she's scrolling through.

### The Read-Aloud Test (book / long-form)

> *Could a friend read this aloud to a group of Practice owners over dinner without it sounding written?*

If the writing reveals itself as writing, it's out of voice. Alex's voice always sounds spoken.

### The Screenshot Test (organic social)

> *Would someone screenshot this and post it to their stories without adding their own commentary?*

If yes, it's in voice for social.

## 17. Anti-Patterns

### Universal (avoid in any register)

- **The accusatory hook.** *"You're losing six figures and you don't even know it."* Replace with Alex's own version of the same realisation in the appropriate energy for the register.
- **The expert tone.** *"Owners who fail to address X will inevitably…"* Replace with *"For years I didn't see this, and what I learned the hard way is…"*
- **The em dash.** Always a tell.
- **Banned phrases** (leverage, harness, delve, game-changer, deep dive, circle back, double down, unpack, at the end of the day).
- **Outsider tells** (customer instead of client, pet instead of patient, exam room instead of consult room).
- **Macho metaphors** (killing it, crushing it, dominating, smashing, battling).
- **Cultural tripwires** (profit-vs-welfare, "Practice owner" without the Vet identity, round numbers, "scale your practice").
- **The Big One** (negation-correction patterns: "not X, but Y").
- **The "as a Vet Practice owner" line.** Talking *to* her in her role rather than *with* her as a person.
- **Forced shape.** Padding or compressing because the template says so, when the topic wants a different length.
- **False urgency.** Fake deadlines, fake scarcity, fake "only 3 spots left." Real urgency only.

### Nurture-register only

- **Hard CTAs.** Nurture earns relationship. Sales does the selling.
- **Pressure cooker urgency.** No countdowns in nurture.
- **Bullet-pointed insight stacks** without story or breathing room.
- **The motivational close.** *"You've got this."* / *"Today's the day."* Replace with quiet honesty.
- **Pattern 21** (Physical decay metaphors). Save for warm retargeting or sales register.

### Sales-register only

- **The wandering open.** Sales emails need to land the stakes in the first three lines.
- **The vague CTA.** Be specific about what she'll get and what happens if she doesn't act by when.
- **Missing the PS.** Alex's sales emails almost always end with a PS that carries real weight. Often the PS is what converts.
- **The over-stacked offer.** Sales offers should be confident in their core, with bonuses sitting under it as proof, not as padding.

### Ad-register only

- **The long setup.** Ads don't get setup. Lead with the confession or the consequence.
- **The unclear ask.** Every ad has one action. Make it obvious.
- **The "swipe →" with no payoff.** If you're asking for the swipe, the swipe has to earn the click.

# PART FIVE: THE REAL-ALEX FILTER & ALEX-ISM LEXICON

*The standing direction: keep the real, story-led Alex above all. Drafts kept reverting to a too-polished version. This part exists to stop that. Run it on every piece.*

---

## 20. The Real-Alex Filter

### The headline rule

Story styling comes first. Looser, more human, more of the real Alex, every time. Polish is the thing we add last and least. When in doubt, leave it rougher and more his. A clean sentence that doesn't sound like Alex is worth less than a slightly untidy one that does.

### What Alex keeps vs what he cuts

When Alex edits a draft, the pattern is consistent. Learn from it.

He keeps: embodied scene detail (the kelpie not waking, 11:42am, the engine off in the driveway), humour, parenthetical asides, his own named stories.

He cuts: standalone empathy lines ("Something to know, gently", "the shame thaws") and structured noticing exercises that read as wordy.

The lesson is not that the empathy is wrong. It is the single highest-converting material for the 65/35 female list. The lesson is how to deliver it so it survives.

### Deliver empathy through scene, not labels

A bare "I see you" sentence reads as soft to Alex and gets trimmed. The same recognition buried inside the specificity of a moment stays in. Carry the feeling in the detail and the parenthetical, not in a visible empathy sentence.

Gets cut: "Something to know, gently. You didn't miss it."

Survives: "(You knew. You've probably known for weeks.)" placed right after the scene that makes it true.

### Think in pictures

Every concept gets one sticky physical metaphor. Table legs for the four pillars. The hat rack for the owner's roles. Coal to diamonds for perturbation. If a teaching beat has no image attached, it is not finished.

### Name the handle

Alex wants a named, ownable concept the reader can carry and repeat, not just the feeling of it. Decision Fatigue. Perturbation. The gap. Give every lesson a name, and a three-word mnemonic where it fits (Pause, Choose, Respond).

### Leave slots for his stories

His real anecdotes (Emma\*, Sarah\*, David Collins\*, the hungry labrador) are the one thing we cannot generate. Do not hand him closed prose with no room to insert himself. Build deliberate gaps where a real story goes. And note that Alex drops proof inside the story (Sarah's\* line lands as social proof mid-narrative), so weave testimonial fragments into the story rather than quarantining them in a separate block.

### Pre-bake the female framing into the body

Alex's empathy for the female reader is real, but his delivery defaults to a male narrator who turns to women as an aside. That aside is where patronisation creeps in on a majority-female list. Build the female-centred framing into the main body as a direct "you" before he ever reaches the draft. Then he has nothing to bolt on.

### Let the humour run

Alex's humour is cheekier and more self-deprecating than our drafts tend to be. Give it room. The slightly absurd line (the labrador with the cooked chicken, the donkey kick) is on-brand, not a risk.

## 21. The Alex-ism Lexicon

*Harvested from Alex's own edits. Season with these where they fit. The point is to sound like him, not to staple every one of these onto every paragraph.*

### Connective tissue and truth-tags
- "Okay... So, let me tell you a story."
- "Truth is, I hadn't missed any of it."
- "Here's what I had to learn the hard way."
- "I'd love to tell you that was the only time. But it wasn't..."
- "Here's what I want to tell you."
- "If I can be really upfront."
- "I can tell you this for certain."
- "Most owners I sit with are technically awesome Vets."
- "Let's replay that same Tuesday..."
- "Tell me more." (his open-question move the moment an owner surfaces a problem)
- "So I'm not recommending anyone does this, by the way..." (the pre-frame before a story he knows is a bit reckless)
- "The likelihood of you doing something wrong is a hundred percent." (blunt truism, stated flat, no hedge)

### Structural tics
- CAPS on a single word for emphasis: THAT, IF, NEVER, ANY, CHOOSING, AFTER.
- Parenthetical deflation and honesty: "(which is never quick and never just a chat)", "(without my help, honestly)", "(my kids are 13 and 17!)", "(sometimes feels like the tax office's advocate!)".
- Slip into present tense mid-scene: "And every single time, I'm driving home asking the same question."
- Italicised inner-voice question: *"How did I miss that?"*
- Trailing ellipsis to let a line hang.
- Hyper-specific numbers and times: 11:42am, the 20 bad minutes versus the 9 good hours, two minutes to spare, three texts.
- Tuesday as the default day things go wrong. It recurs on purpose.
- Double-tap repetition to land a point: "Everyone loses. Everyone loses."
- Flow-sound language for things ticking over smoothly: "ch-ch-ch", "tic-tacking along".
